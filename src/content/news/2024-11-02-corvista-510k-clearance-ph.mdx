**TORONTO, November 2, 2024** — CorVista Health announced today that the U.S. Food and Drug Administration has granted 510(k) clearance for the CorVista System for the assessment of pulmonary hypertension (PH) in symptomatic patients. The clearance follows the company's FDA 510(k) clearance for coronary artery disease (CAD) received in 2023 and a Breakthrough Device Designation granted by the FDA in 2022.

The CorVista System is now the only FDA-cleared device capable of assessing for both CAD and PH from a single, non-invasive, resting capture at the point of care.

"This second clearance marks a fundamental expansion of what a front-line cardiovascular test can do," said Charles R. Bridges, M.D., Sc.D., EVP and Chief Scientific Officer of CorVista Health. "Pulmonary hypertension is frequently missed for years because the tools to identify it simply weren't available outside of specialist centers. That changes today."

PH affects an estimated 1% of the global population and up to 10% of patients over 65. The condition is associated with a median survival of less than three years from diagnosis when left untreated, yet the average time from symptom onset to diagnosis has historically exceeded two years.

### Clinical validation

The FDA submission was supported by data from the company's pivotal PH validation study, which enrolled 2,409 patients across 19 clinical sites. Patients underwent CorVista System evaluation followed by right-heart catheterization (RHC), the gold-standard diagnostic for PH. All CorVista readings were blinded to RHC results.

- Sensitivity: 82%
- Specificity: 92%
- Negative predictive value: >99%

### About the CorVista System

The CorVista System captures 220 seconds of resting cardiovascular biosignals using non-invasive adhesive sensors placed on the patient's chest and back. Signals are processed by proprietary AI algorithms trained on tens of thousands of paired catheterization cases and returned to the ordering physician as a structured, physician-reviewed report — typically within minutes.

No radiation, contrast, exercise, or needles are required. The test can be administered by a medical assistant in any clinical setting, including primary care offices, community hospitals, and cardiology practices.
